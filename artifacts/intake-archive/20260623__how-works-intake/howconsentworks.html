Waxing & waning: the missing variable

“Waxing” and “waning” are directional claims. A single moment can tell you a state, but it cannot tell you a direction without a second moment—or without a shared model of motion.

In dynamic systems, the safest-seeming moment can be the most dangerous: the peak. Peaks are where reversal happens, where comfort tempts assumption.

Loop-safe rule: Never act at the peak of consent unless you are confident the direction is still ≥ 0 (waxing or steady).

A minimal consent variable

Let consent be a bounded process:

C(t) ∈ [0,1]

  • dC/dt > 0 → consent is waxing
  • dC/dt < 0 → consent is waning

The value of C and the direction of C are independent. Ethics lives in that gap.

Trust is not stored.
It is synchronized.

Trust behaves like coupled loops: my trust in you, your trust in me, trust in the context, trust in repair. In coupled systems, breakdown often happens not at “low trust,” but at phase misalignment.

Common failure mode

One person believes trust is waxing (“we’re getting closer”), while the other experiences it as waning (“I’m getting tired / pressured / unseen”). Snapshots hide the mismatch.

Repair tends to occur in a specific region: low but waxing trust. That’s where vulnerability is metabolizable, signals are legible, and agency is preserved.

Three danger cases (direction > level)

  • High consent, waning → imminent violation risk
  • Low consent, waxing → repair opportunity
  • Medium consent, oscillating → fragile negotiation zone

A binary “yes/no” interface cannot represent these regions, which is why many systems unintentionally pressure people into oversimplification.

What a consent-aware system must do

To respect living dynamics, design must surface direction, protect pause, privilege reversibility, and reveal phase mismatch without blame.

  • Direction: Is willingness increasing, decreasing, or stabilizing?
  • Pause without penalty: Stopping must not incur social debt.
  • Reversibility: Changing one’s mind must remain safe and dignified.
  • Aftercare / memory: Every loop pass updates baseline and elasticity.
Pin-worthy axiom: Consent is not compliance; it is a living signal. Trust is not a score; it is a relationship that must remain phase-aligned.

Practice: listen for motion

Try questions that detect direction without pressure:

  • “Is this feeling easier, harder, or the same as a minute ago?”
  • “Do you want more of this, less of this, or a pause?”
  • “Is your ‘yes’ growing, shrinking, or steady?”
  • “If we stop right now, will that feel safe and clean?”

The goal is not persuasion. The goal is alignment—so the loop stays alive.

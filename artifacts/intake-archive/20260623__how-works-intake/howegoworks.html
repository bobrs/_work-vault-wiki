The Day You Let Go of the Handlebars

(and why “being aligned” is not a real plan)

Reading time: ~6–8 min • Tone: irreverent + precise • Jump to the punchline
Intro The Lie What’s Going On Personalities Growth Grenades Reframe Punchline

At some point, if you ride a bike long enough, you take your hands off the handlebars.

Not because you’re reckless.
Not because you’re enlightened.
But because you accidentally discover that the bike was doing most of the work already.

It’s exhilarating.
Also terrifying.
Also very hard to explain to people who have a tight emotional relationship with handlebars.

Life is like that, except instead of a quiet road, people are throwing melons, nails, and—on some days—actual grenades.

The Lie We Were Sold

The self-help industry will tell you that growth is about becoming:

  • better
  • more authentic
  • aligned
  • healed
  • optimized
  • finally your “true self”

This sounds nice. It also explains absolutely nothing.

Because none of those words tell you how control is actually happening.

They’re just compliments we give to whatever coping strategy didn’t kill us.

What’s Actually Going On (No Crystals Required)

Most of the time, you are not consciously steering your life.

Your body, habits, timing, tone, and intuition are doing high-speed micro-corrections long before your thoughts show up. This is good. This is how humans work.

Call this hands-free riding.

Sometimes, though, something goes wrong:

  • the road changes
  • the speed increases
  • someone lobs a grenade into your week

When that happens, you grab the handlebars.

You slow down.
You focus.
You think.
You tighten control.

Also good. Also necessary.

The problem is not grabbing the handlebars.
The problem is never letting go again.

How Personalities Are Born (This Is the Part People Don’t Like)

Now add one more ingredient:

A fucked up life.

Unpredictable parents.
Unstable money.
Unsafe relationships.
Chronic pressure.
Early chaos.

In those conditions, grabbing the handlebars isn’t optional — it’s survival.

So the system learns something very reasonable:

“This is the only place safety exists.”

And then it stays there.

That frozen strategy hardens. It gets decorated. It gets justified. It gets called personality.

Control freaks. People-pleasers. Avoidants. Chaos-seekers. Mystics. Nihilists. Hyper-rationalists. Even the ones who swear they chose it “on purpose.”

Same physics.
Different blast radii.

Growth Is Not What You Think It Is

Here’s the heresy:

Growth, healing, maturity — whatever word you want — is not:

  • becoming better
  • becoming authentic
  • becoming aligned

Those are just flattering stories we tell about whatever loop happens to be running.

Actual growth is much less poetic:

Growth is restoring the ability to choose how much control you’re using.

That’s it.

Not removing control.
Not transcending control.
Not surrendering forever.

Just… choice.

About Grenades

Some days really do require full control.

Emergencies happen.
Boundaries matter.
Decisions must be made fast and hard.

This is not a failure of flow. It’s physics.

The mistake is assuming the world is always a battlefield.

If you never release control after the blast clears, you don’t become safer.

You become smaller.

The Quiet Reframe

So here’s a better question than “Am I healed?” or “Am I aligned?”:

Do I still have access to more than one way of steering?

If yes, you’re doing fine — even if your life is messy.
If no, no amount of affirmations will help.

The Punchline

Most of what we call personality
is just a control system
that never got the memo
that the blast radius ended.

And most of what we call growth
is simply learning
that it’s safe to loosen your grip again.

Not forever.
Not blindly.
Just… when the road allows it.

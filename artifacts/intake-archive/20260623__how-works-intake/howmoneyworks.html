Money is a coordination technology.

It lets strangers cooperate by compressing complex value into a single number. That’s powerful — and it can also quietly price things that were never meant to be sold: attention, care, presence, and time.

Core idea: money works best when it stays a tool. Trouble starts when it becomes the truth about what matters.

What is money?

Money is a shared agreement that certain tokens (coins, notes, bank balances) can stand in for value. It’s not “value” itself — it’s a representational layer.

Function What it does Why it matters
Medium of exchange Lets you trade without bartering (no “double coincidence of wants”). Enables specialization and trade at scale.
Unit of account Creates a common measuring stick for prices and costs. Makes planning, budgeting, and comparison possible.
Store of value Lets you carry purchasing power through time. Supports saving — but also incentivizes hoarding and rent-seeking if misdesigned.

Money is…

  • Social memory (who can claim what)
  • A ledger (even when it’s paper)
  • Portable trust (accepted by others)
  • A language (prices are sentences)

When money becomes the only language, we lose nuance. Not everything that’s real is measurable, and not everything measurable is worth maximizing.

Why money enables scale

Pre-money communities scaled by relationship: kinship, reputation, rituals, shared stories. That’s high-fidelity — but it’s bandwidth-limited.

Money reduces coordination friction scale
  • Strangers can trade without deep trust.
  • Markets can match supply/demand rapidly.
  • Specialization rises because exchange is easier.
Money creates a “universal comparer” measurement
  • Different goods become comparable via price.
  • That powers accounting, contracts, and planning.
  • But it can also collapse multi-dimensional value into one number.

Hidden costs: what money can do to meaning

Money doesn’t just enable exchange — it can change what people think is worth doing. When the scoreboard becomes reality, we start optimizing for the scoreboard.

Value collapse unit-of-account side effect

Many kinds of value (care, presence, dignity) get flattened into price. What can’t be priced becomes “invisible.”

Extraction incentives power

If profit is the only signal, systems drift toward capturing attention, time, and dependency — even when everyone privately hates it.

Coordination miracles real benefits

Medicine supply chains, infrastructure, global collaboration — money can coordinate enormous efforts when paired with good institutions.

So is money “good” or “bad”? lens

It’s neither. Money is a protocol for allocating claims on resources. The question is: what values does the protocol enforce? And: where does consent belong?

How money is created (modern version)

In most modern economies, a lot of “money” is created as credit — i.e., bank deposits that exist because someone borrowed and someone else is owed.

Three big creators mechanics
  • Central bank: sets policy rates, provides reserves/liquidity, influences conditions.
  • Commercial banks: create deposits when they make loans (credit creation).
  • Government (fiscal): spends/taxes/borrows; interacts with banking + central bank architecture.
What “printing money” usually means myth-busting
  • Often it’s not literal printing — it’s balance sheets: reserves, bonds, deposits.
  • Inflation depends on supply, demand, constraints, expectations, and policy — not just “more digits exist.”

My time & consent

A common pain point is not “money exists,” but the quieter claim that your time is a commodity by default. If that feels like a boundary breach, you’re not broken — you’re noticing a design choice.

Two ways to relate to time

Time as commodity market lens
  • Time is priced as an input to production.
  • “Opportunity cost” becomes a constant background hum.
  • Risk: you start treating yourself as inventory.
Time as lived continuity human lens
  • Time is the medium of your life, not a unit for sale.
  • Some acts are “priceless” not as cliché — but as category boundary.
  • Consent belongs before valuation.

A consent-aware prompt you can use

Before you say yes to a priced demand, ask:

  • 1 Is this aligned with my values, or just the scoreboard?
  • 2 What boundary am I being asked to move?
  • 3 Can I offer a different form of value (care, creativity, clarity) without commodifying my being?
  • 4 What would a fully consensual “yes” feel like in my body?
Boundary note: “Not for sale” is a valid category. You don’t owe the market an explanation.

Practical tools (without moralizing)

If money is a coordination layer, you can choose which layer you’re on — or when you step off it.

Personal level practice
  • Separate price from worth: track both.
  • Budget for “non-market time” as a protected resource.
  • Rename categories: “care,” “play,” “repair,” “learning,” not just “free time.”
Community level design
  • Mutual aid / gift circles / time-banks (with consent-first boundaries).
  • Local credit ledgers where meaning isn’t forced into one number.
  • Explicit norms: what is never priced (presence, certain care acts, etc.).

Glossary

  • Credit: a promise (an asset for one party, a liability for another).
  • Inflation: broad increase in prices (often “too much demand chasing too little supply,” but details matter).
  • Liquidity: ease of paying/settling now.
  • Rent-seeking: extracting payment without creating proportional value.
  • Externality: cost/benefit not reflected in the price.

The claim Implication Principle

How audit works.

A short page about a simple paradox that becomes a design constraint the moment you take consent seriously.

Formal stipulation

You can’t audit consent without consent.

What this means

Auditing requires access: to records, relationships, metadata, interpretations, and judgments. That access is itself a use.

  • If the audit is not consented to, the audit violates the very thing it claims to verify.
  • So an “unconsented audit of consent” is self-contradictory.

Why this matters

Many systems treat consent as an artifact: a checkbox, a signature, a terms acceptance. But consent is also a relationship with boundaries.

  • Post-hoc inspection is not legitimacy.
  • Verification must be part of the consented process, not imposed from outside it.
Principle:

Any attempt to verify consent is itself a use that must be consented to. If inference is not consent-gated, consent is symbolic.

Practical implications (short) ＋
1) Audit becomes a protocol step
The audit is not an after-the-fact inspection. It is a consented interaction with defined scope.
2) Scope and revocation apply to auditors
Auditors must be subject to the same constraints as any other user of the information.
3) “We can verify later” is a smell
If you can only verify consent by violating it, your system is not consent-native.
A line you can use in conversation ＋
Stipulation:
“Consent fails when people agree to the same words but mean different things.”
Follow-on (only if invited):
“So audits that assume consent is a static artifact miss the real failure mode: meaning mismatch and unconsented use.”

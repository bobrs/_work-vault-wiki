The AI Control Map

When you talk to an AI, a handful of controllable elements decide what you get back. This module focuses on the ones you can directly influence — in plain language.

1. Instruction

What you ask for: the task, goal, and constraints. Vague instruction is the #1 cause of disappointing outputs.

2. Structure

The requested format, steps, and headings. Structure tells the AI how to organize its answer so it’s usable for you.

3. Style & Tone

How it should sound: concise vs. detailed, formal vs. casual, technical vs. simple. Style massively changes usefulness.

4. Examples

Concrete samples of what “good” looks like. Examples let the AI imitate exactly the flavor and shape you want.

5. Session State

What’s already been said, what’s still relevant, and when to reset. Good sessions are well-maintained sessions.

6. Feedback

How you respond to outputs: accept, refine, or correct. Precise feedback teaches the AI how to course-correct fast.

Module 1

Instruction: Say What You Actually Want

Most people describe a topic. You’re going to describe a task.

Weak prompt
“Tell me about marketing funnels.”
Controlled prompt
“You are helping me design a simple 3-step marketing funnel for a small online course. Goal: outline the funnel and tell me what to do at each step. Constraints: - Audience: solo creators with limited time - Channel: email + one social platform - Output: bullet list with 3 sections (Top / Middle / Bottom), each with: - goal - 2–3 example actions - 1 metric to watch”
  • State the goal (“design”, “debug”, “outline”, “compare”…)
  • Give 2–3 key constraints (who it’s for, where it lives, limits)
  • Say what the output should look like (bullets, steps, table, etc.)
Try this now — swap in your topic
“You are helping me <ACTION> about <TOPIC>. Goal: <WHAT YOU WANT DONE>. Constraints: - Audience: <WHO IT’S FOR> - Context: <WHERE/HOW IT’S USED> - Limits: <WHAT TO AVOID OR KEEP SHORT> Output: <FORMAT YOU WANT (e.g., 5 bullet points, a 3-step plan, etc.)>”
Module 2

Structure: Shape How the Answer Arrives

Structure turns a generic answer into something you can plug directly into your work.

With no structure
“Explain this contract to me in simple terms.”

You’ll get a wall of text.

With clear structure
“Explain this contract to me in simple terms. Structure your answer in 4 sections, with headings: 1. ‘Summary in 3 bullets’ 2. ‘What I am responsible for’ 3. ‘What the other party is responsible for’ 4. ‘Risks or things to watch’ In each section, use short bullet points.”
  • Tell the AI how many sections or steps you want
  • Give each section a heading so you can skim
  • Specify bullets / numbered steps / tables explicitly
Template — describe the format before you send
“Answer in this format: 1. <HEADING 1> - <bullet> - <bullet> 2. <HEADING 2> - <bullet> 3. <HEADING 3> - <bullet> Keep each bullet under 20 words.”
Module 3

Style & Tone: Make It Sound Useful

Same information, different tone = different usability. You control this up front.

Style options you can name
“Explain this: - as if to a smart 14-year-old” - in concise bullet points only - in 3 levels: basic / intermediate / expert - in a friendly but professional tone - using examples from <YOUR DOMAIN>
  • Audience: who is this for?
  • Density: brief overview vs. deep dive?
  • Vibe: formal report, casual note, or something in between?
Try this combo
“Rewrite the explanation you just gave: - Audience: busy non-technical founder - Tone: calm, confident, practical - Format: 5 bullets total - Constraint: no sentence longer than 18 words”
Module 4

Session Control: Reset, Refine, and Steer

Good AI work is iterative. You’re allowed — encouraged — to steer as you go.

Mid-conversation steering phrases
“Summarize what we’re doing in one sentence.” “Restate my goal in your own words.” “You’re focusing too much on X. Shift to Y instead.” “Stop. Propose 3 alternative directions we could take next.”
Resetting when things drift
“We’ve drifted from my goal. Ignore all previous details. New goal: <STATE IT>. Based only on this new goal, suggest a clean 3-step plan.”

When conversation gets messy, don’t wrestle it. Reset and re-state the goal clearly.

  • Ask the AI to summarize your shared goal
  • Call out what’s off (“too generic”, “too theoretical”, “missing examples”)
  • Start a clean thread for large pivots

Quickstart Checklist

Before you send any important AI prompt, run this 15-second check. It alone will dramatically improve your results.

  • Goal: Did I say what I want done, not just the topic?
  • Constraints: Did I mention audience, context, and limits?
  • Format: Did I specify bullets / steps / sections?
  • Style: Did I set tone and depth?
  • Next move: Do I know what I’ll ask for after the first answer?
Copy-paste starter
“You are helping me <ACTION> about <TOPIC>. Goal: - <WHAT YOU WANT DONE> Constraints: - Audience: <WHO THIS IS FOR> - Context: <WHERE/HOW THIS IS USED> - Limit: <WHAT TO KEEP SHORT / AVOID> Output: - Format: <BULLETS / STEPS / SECTIONS> - Style: <TONE + DEPTH>

FAQ

Is this about one specific AI tool?

No. These principles apply to any modern text-based AI assistant. The knobs and buttons may look different, but the underlying behavior is similar: it continues whatever pattern you set.

Do I need to understand how the model works inside?

Not for this module. Think of this as learning the controls on the dashboard, not rebuilding the engine. If you can write plain English, you can use this.

Can I share or adapt this module?

Yes. You’re welcome to share, remix, and customize the templates for your team, students, or clients. Just keep it free and keep the spirit: help people get better results, safely.

How does this connect to “inner work” or personal development?

That part is optional. Many people notice that the skills are similar: when you get better at stating goals, setting constraints, and giving feedback to AI, you often get better at doing the same with yourself and others. But this module stays focused on AI behavior.
